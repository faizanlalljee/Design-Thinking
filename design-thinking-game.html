Phase 01 ‚Äî Empathize

Understand Your Users

Before you design anything, you need to deeply understand the people you're designing for ‚Äî their needs, frustrations, and the context of their lives.

What is Empathy in Design? Core Concept

Empathy is the foundation of human-centered design. It means setting aside your own assumptions and genuinely trying to understand the world through your user's eyes ‚Äî their emotions, motivations, and real behaviours, not just what they say they do.

üëÅ
Observe
Watch users in their natural environment without interfering. Behaviour reveals what words often hide.
üéô
Engage
Ask open-ended "why" questions. Listen more than you speak. Let silences breathe.
üîÑ
Immerse
Experience the problem yourself if possible. Walk the same path your users walk.
üö´
Suspend Judgment
Resist the urge to solve. Your only job at this stage is to understand, not fix.

"You can't innovate for people you don't understand."

‚Äî IDEO Design Thinking Principle
‚ö†Ô∏è
Common mistake: Jumping straight to surveys. Surveys tell you what people think they feel ‚Äî not what they actually do. The gap between the two is where design insight lives.
‚úÖ
Best practice: Shadowing (following someone through their experience) is one of the most powerful empathy tools. It surfaces invisible pain points the user has learned to accept.

Phase 02 ‚Äî Define

Frame the Problem

Empathy gives you raw material. Defining transforms that material into a sharp, actionable problem statement ‚Äî your north star for everything that follows.

What is a Point of View (POV)? Core Concept

A Point of View statement is a human-centered problem definition. It captures who your user is, what they need, and the insight that explains why that need matters. It's not a solution ‚Äî it's a reframing that opens up creative possibilities.

üßë
Name the User
Be specific. "A first-time hospital visitor" is better than "people." Specificity creates empathy.
üéØ
State the Need
Express a need, not a feature. "Navigate with confidence" not "have a better map."
üí°
Add the Insight
The surprising "because" ‚Äî the root cause that makes the need feel urgent and human.
üîç
Synthesise Patterns
Look across all your observations. What themes keep appearing? What surprised you most?

"If I had an hour to solve a problem, I'd spend 55 minutes thinking about the problem and 5 minutes thinking about solutions."

‚Äî Often attributed to Albert Einstein
‚ö†Ô∏è
Common mistake: Writing a solution-focused problem statement. "We need a better app" is not a problem definition ‚Äî it's already a solution. Stay in the problem space.
‚úÖ
Formula to remember: [User] needs a way to [need] because [insight]. The insight is the most important part ‚Äî it's what makes the problem feel real and urgent.

Phase 03 ‚Äî Ideate

Generate Bold Ideas

Ideation is a creative explosion. The rules are different here ‚Äî volume beats quality, wild beats safe, and judgment is strictly forbidden.

The Art of Divergent Thinking Core Concept

Most people try to find the right answer immediately. Design thinkers do the opposite ‚Äî they deliberately generate many imperfect answers first. This divergent phase is followed by convergence (picking the best ideas), but you can't converge well without first expanding the possibility space.

üöÄ
Defer Judgment
No critiquing during brainstorming. "Yes, and‚Ä¶" not "No, because‚Ä¶". Every idea is valid right now.
üìä
Go for Volume
Aim for 20+ ideas before filtering. Research shows that breakthrough ideas typically appear later in a session.
üåÄ
How Might We
Reframe problems as opportunities. "How might we make navigation feel like a game?" opens more doors than "Fix the signs."
ü§ù
Build on Others
Use others' ideas as springboards. "Yes, and‚Ä¶" compounding turns one idea into ten.

"The best way to have a good idea is to have lots of ideas."

‚Äî Linus Pauling, Nobel Prize winner
‚ö†Ô∏è
Common mistake: Converging too soon. Teams often jump to refining one idea before exploring the full solution space. The most obvious first idea is rarely the best one.
‚úÖ
Technique: Try "Crazy 8s" ‚Äî sketch 8 wildly different ideas in 8 minutes. Speed forces your brain past the obvious and into the unexpected.

Phase 04 ‚Äî Prototype

Build to Learn

A prototype is not a finished product ‚Äî it's a question with a physical form. You build it to learn, not to launch.

Why Prototype Early and Often? Core Concept

Prototyping is about making ideas tangible so you can test your assumptions quickly and cheaply. The faster you put something in front of users, the faster you learn what works ‚Äî and what doesn't. The goal is never polish; it's insight.

üìÑ
Start Low-Fidelity
Paper, cardboard, sticky notes. Low-fidelity means faster iteration and users focus on the concept, not the aesthetics.
‚ùì
Prototype One Thing
Each prototype should answer one key question. Don't build everything ‚Äî build the part you're most uncertain about.
üí∏
Fail Cheap
A failed paper prototype costs 2 hours. A failed polished app costs months. Front-load your failures.
üîÅ
Iterate Fast
Build ‚Üí Test ‚Üí Learn ‚Üí Build again. Each cycle makes your understanding sharper.

"Build to think, not think to build."

‚Äî Tom Kelley, IDEO
‚ö†Ô∏è
Common mistake: Falling in love with your prototype. When you've spent days building something, you stop seeing its flaws. Emotional attachment is the enemy of objective learning.
‚úÖ
Types of prototypes: Paper sketches, storyboards, clickable wireframes, role-playing, physical models, and "Wizard of Oz" simulations (a human manually mimics a system). Choose the method that answers your key question fastest.

Phase 05 ‚Äî Test

Test & Iterate

Testing is not a final exam ‚Äî it's a continuous learning loop. Every session teaches you something that makes your next version better.

How to Test Like a Design Thinker Core Concept

The goal of a test session is NOT to prove your design works. It's to discover how it fails. When users struggle, get confused, or take unexpected paths ‚Äî those are your most valuable data points. Embrace failure; it's teaching you what to do next.

ü§´
Stay Silent
Resist the urge to explain or help. When users struggle, let them. That struggle is your data.
üëÄ
Watch, Don't Ask
Observe what users do, not just what they say. Behaviour and reported behaviour often diverge sharply.
üìù
Capture Everything
Note hesitations, wrong turns, facial expressions, unexpected uses. The richest insight is often in the margins.
üîÑ
Iterate, Don't Defend
When users fail, update the design ‚Äî don't explain it better. If it needs explaining, it needs redesigning.

"Testing is not about validation. It's about learning what you don't yet know."

‚Äî Stanford d.school
‚ö†Ô∏è
Common mistake: Treating a partial success as a green light. If 2 out of 3 users succeed, you haven't learned why ‚Äî or whether your solution will scale. The failures contain the real lesson.
‚úÖ
Key mindset: Test sessions are not about the prototype ‚Äî they're about the user. When users behave unexpectedly, don't correct them. Follow their logic. It might reveal a better design than yours.

Design Thinking Cheat Sheet

Empathize: Observe first, ask later. Shadow real users. Avoid surveys that confirm your biases.
Define: A great POV is human-centered, not solution-focused. Name the person, their need, and the insight behind it.
Ideate: Defer judgment. Use "How Might We". Aim for 20+ ideas before filtering. Build on others.
Prototype: Build rough, cheap, and fast. The goal is to learn ‚Äî not to impress. Paper beats Figma in early rounds.
Test: You're testing the prototype, not the user. Stay silent. Watch. Iterate relentlessly ‚Äî one insight can reshape everything.
